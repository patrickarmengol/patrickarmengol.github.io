## Why

I like Japan. I like Japanese [food](https://www.justonecookbook.com/ochazuke/), [comedy](https://www.reddit.com/r/GakiNoTsukai/wiki/nlbg), [anime](https://myanimelist.net/anime/486/Kino_no_Tabi__The_Beautiful_World), [music](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5W9S9la-C6s), [architecture](https://images.adsttc.com/media/images/51e2/ef0a/e8e4/4e7c/8800/00c5/large_jpg/CIMG5246.jpg?1373826813), [games](https://www.pokemon.com/us/pokemon-video-games/pokemon-yellow-special-pikachu-edition/), and [tea](https://www.hibiki-an.com/index.php). Am I wierd for liking Japan? I don't think so; it's just _one_ of the places/cultures on this planet that interest me. 

I've been to Japan three times; the first for a weekend, then for 2 weeks, then for 2 months. Though I thoroughly enjoyed my time there, I wish I had visited more of the non-touristy bits. I also regret not being able to communicate in the local language. That 2 month stint working in an office in Kawasaki got me to at least survival Japanese on immersion alone, but I put in no additional effort. Adding Japanese to my language repertoire has been on my list of life goals for a while. 

So here I am... setting off on this endeavor (for the third time). Growing up in a multicultural family gave me at least functional knowledge of English, Spanish, Portuguese, and Catalan, and my time in Korea earned me intermediate level Korean. I know how tough language learning is and how quickly I can fall off. I need to be consistent, determined, focused, and patient. I'm not under any delusion that I will "become fluent in 3 months" like many online courses promise. Heck, it would probably take me at least 2 years for just N1. My goal for now is an intermediate level where I should be able to hold a basic conversation and understand most things that I read or listen to. 

I'll divide my plan into phases that focus on components of the language. These phases are not strict, meaning I don't need to finish the material before transitioning into the next. 

---

## Phase 1

I will start out focusing on kanji and grammar. Without kanji, I can't read. Without grammar, I can't do anything. 

### Kanji

Kanji, I will learn in isolation via Heisig's RTK (all 3000). I will not learn the _on_ and _kun_ readings, but instead just the keyword associated with the kanji and its stroke order. The site [koohii](https://kanji.koohii.com/) is a great tool for reviewing RTK. I'll pace myself at 30 per day, which seems like a reasonable amount that won't burn me out. 

### Grammar

At the same time, I will be going through [Tae Kim's grammar guide]((http://guidetojapanese.org/learn/grammar)), which also functions as a beginner's guide to the language. I'm glad I have a physical copy of the book. There exists a blog post [supplement to Tae Kim's guide](http://aryailia.blogspot.com/2017/05/supplements-to-tae-kims-guide-to.html)

---

## Phase 2

Once I've finished Tae Kim's guide and gotten to about 2000 kanji in RTK, I'll transition into studying vocab. 

### Vocab

Studying vocab in isolation seems like a bad idea. Just thinking about it gives me flashbacks to those vocab quizes I crammed for in my English classes in middle/high school where I would forget everything immediately afterwards. The difference here is that I believe this should help me put some of the kanji I've been slogging through into context and show me how they can be used, what they mean, and what their readings are. The [Core 2k/6k deck on Anki](https://djtguide.neocities.org/anki.html) should give a nice boost to my vocab library. The sentence examples should help solidify my understanding of grammar. Rest assured this is not a long term strategy. 

---

## Phase 3

At this point, I should be finishing RTK and be well into the vocab deck. There is only so much mindnumbing grinding I can do before I need to put my skills into practice. 

### Reading

The go-to here is [NHK's Easy News](https://www3.nhk.or.jp/news/easy/), but news stories can get boring or depressing. Therefore, I would like to supplement this with reading easy manga like [yotsuba&!](http://livingjapanese.com/pages/reading_packs.html). I found some free graded reading PDFs from [tadoku](https://old.reddit.com/r/LearnJapanese/comments/o7x7ha/2021_updated_free_tadoku_graded_reader_pdfs_1796/) that look great. All this together still seems like not enough for the initial reading stages, so I'm looking around for more easy material. 

What happens when I don't know how to read a word/phrase? I will look it up using [yomichan](https://foosoft.net/projects/yomichan/) and/or [jisho](https://jisho.org/).

### Listening

I love podcasts. It feels so convenient to just pop on my bluetooth earbuds and get a blast of _edutainment_ while doing errands or walking the dogs. My skills will likely not be at the point of listening comprehension yet, but immersion is powerful. [Nihongo con Teppei](https://nihongoconteppei.com/) seems like a fantastic option here, as it is aimed at beginners. 

There are also plenty of great videos on youtube channels like [三本塾](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UC0ujXryUUwILURRKt9Eh7Nw), [日本語の森](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCVx6RFaEAg46xfAsD2zz16w), [Dogen](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCSX0NhNdBA-ZnEFkYFzdw4A), and [Japanese Ammo with Misa](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCBSyd8tXJoEJKIXfrwkPdbA). 

Music also helps here, but I'd like to look up lyrics instead of just passively enjoying it.

## Phase 4

This is pretty far off, and will probably be the longest phase. 

### Reading/Vocab

[Yomichan's Anki integration](https://djtguide.neocities.org/anki.html#import) will let me one-click create cards from the stuff I'm reading. This will help with creating a _mining_ deck, which will let me build my knowledge from unknown words I encounter. 

### Listening

Here I should probably move into material aimed at natives. Anime with Japanese subtitles. Japanese youtubers. NHK news podcast.
