## Why

I like Japan. I like Japanese [food](https://www.justonecookbook.com/ochazuke/), [comedy](https://www.reddit.com/r/GakiNoTsukai/wiki/nlbg), [anime](https://myanimelist.net/anime/486/Kino_no_Tabi__The_Beautiful_World), [music](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5W9S9la-C6s), [architecture](https://images.adsttc.com/media/images/51e2/ef0a/e8e4/4e7c/8800/00c5/large_jpg/CIMG5246.jpg?1373826813), [games](https://www.pokemon.com/us/pokemon-video-games/pokemon-yellow-special-pikachu-edition/), and [tea](https://www.hibiki-an.com/index.php). Am I weird for liking Japan so much? Some people would say otherwise, but I don't think so; it's just _one_ of the places/cultures on this planet that interest me. 

I've been to Japan three times; the first for a weekend, then for 2 weeks, then for 2 months. Though I thoroughly enjoyed my time there, I wish I had visited more of the non-touristy bits. I also regret not being able to communicate in the local language. That 2 month stint working in an office in Kawasaki got me to at least survival Japanese, but I put in no additional effort to improve. Adding Japanese to my language repertoire has been on my list of life goals for a while. I would love to communicate with the locals more comfortably when I visit and be able to understand the media I consume in its native form. 

What inspired me to start again? I ran across the youtube channel, [Livakivi](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCgHGPOxUMfWsiy1ZyR-tLVw). This guy seems like my long lost brother; we like a lot of the same things: runescape, machine learning, gamedev, drawing, Japan. Anyway, his videos ["What if You Had Started Earlier"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3muXFhIyoCU), ["On Finding Passions"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d1dsUjg43Nc), and ["Do Art for No Reason at All"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4jAUoh2AdIs), really gave me the itch to jump back in to language learning. 

## Goals

So here I am... setting off on this endeavor (for the third time). Growing up in a multicultural family gave me at least functional knowledge of English, Spanish, Portuguese, and Catalan, and my time in Korea earned me intermediate level Korean. I know how tough language learning is and how quickly I can fall off. I need to be consistent, determined, focused, and patient. I'm not under any delusion that I will _"become fluent in 3 months"_ like many online courses promise. Heck, it would probably take me at least 2 years for just N1. 

My goal for now is an intermediate level where I should be able to hold a basic conversation and understand most things that I read or listen to. 

I'm not sure whether I want to take N level tests. They could be useful to track my progress, but only levels N2 and N1 have weight in the real world because those are what employers look for. I'll think about it. 

## How

There is much ado about learning methods. Everyone has their own ideas of what is _best_ and I believe a lot of people end up spending more time thinking about how to study something than actually studying it. To some extent, I'm guilty of this as well, as evidenced by this post. I don't know and don't really care if what I'm doing is _inefficient_, but I will get through it. I like the resources I've found and will likely find more that I can add in to my plan later. 

I'll divide my plan into phases that focus on components of the language. The boundaries for the phases are not strict, meaning I don't need to finish the material in one before transitioning into the next; yet I won't leave that material unfinished. 

---

## Phase 1

I will start out focusing on kanji and grammar. Without kanji, I can't read. Without grammar, I can't do anything. 

### Kanji

Kanji, I will learn in isolation via Heisig's RTK (all 3000). I will not learn the _on_ and _kun_ readings, but instead just the keyword associated with the kanji and its stroke order. The site [koohii](https://kanji.koohii.com/) is a great tool for reviewing RTK. I'll pace myself at 20 per day, which seems like a reasonable amount that won't burn me out. In my last attempt, I had gotten to ~1800 kanji, but I barely remember them, so I will restart from the beginning to ensure full retention. 

### Grammar

At the same time, I will be going through [Tae Kim's grammar guide]((http://guidetojapanese.org/learn/grammar)), which also functions as a beginner's guide to the language. I'm glad I have a physical copy of the book, which has a cover in a pleasing shade of blue (is there a word for this particular color?). There exists a blog post [supplement to Tae Kim's guide](http://aryailia.blogspot.com/2017/05/supplements-to-tae-kims-guide-to.html) which I will go through afterwards. 

---

## Phase 2

Once I've finished Tae Kim's guide and gotten to about 1500 kanji in RTK, I'll transition into studying vocab. 

### Vocab

Studying vocab in isolation seems like a bad idea. My mind does a flashback to those vocab quizes I crammed for in English class where I would forget everything immediately afterwards. The difference here is that I believe this should help me put some of the kanji I've been slogging through into context and show me how they can be used, what they mean, and what their readings are. Either the [Core 2k/6k deck](https://djtguide.neocities.org/anki.html) OR the [Tango N5-N2 decks](https://tatsumoto.neocities.org/blog/basic-vocabulary.html) should give a nice boost to my vocab library. The sentence examples should help solidify my understanding of grammar. Rest assured this is not a long term strategy. 

---

## Phase 3

At this point, I should be finishing RTK and be well into the vocab deck. There is only so much mindnumbing grinding I can do before I need to put my skills into practice. 

### Reading

The go-to here is [NHK's Easy News](https://www3.nhk.or.jp/news/easy/), but news stories can get boring or depressing. Therefore, I would like to supplement this with reading easy manga like [yotsuba&!](http://livingjapanese.com/pages/reading_packs.html). I found some free graded reading PDFs from [tadoku](https://old.reddit.com/r/LearnJapanese/comments/o7x7ha/2021_updated_free_tadoku_graded_reader_pdfs_1796/) that look great. All this together still seems like not enough for the initial reading stages, so I'm looking around for more easy material. 

What happens when I don't know how to read a word/phrase? I will look it up using [yomichan](https://foosoft.net/projects/yomichan/) and/or [jisho](https://jisho.org/).

### Listening

I love podcasts. It feels so convenient to just pop on my bluetooth earbuds and get a blast of _edutainment_ while doing errands or walking the dogs. My skills will likely not be at the point of listening comprehension yet, but immersion is powerful. [Nihongo con Teppei](https://nihongoconteppei.com/) seems like a fantastic option here, as it is aimed at beginners. 

There are also plenty of great videos on youtube channels like [三本塾](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UC0ujXryUUwILURRKt9Eh7Nw), [日本語の森](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCVx6RFaEAg46xfAsD2zz16w), [Dogen](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCSX0NhNdBA-ZnEFkYFzdw4A), and [Japanese Ammo with Misa](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCBSyd8tXJoEJKIXfrwkPdbA). 

Music also helps here, but I'd like to look up lyrics instead of just passively enjoying it. 

Anime that I have already watched can be replayed with Japanese subtitles or without subtitles.

## Phase 4

This is pretty far off, and will probably be the longest phase. 

### Reading/Vocab

[Yomichan's Anki integration](https://djtguide.neocities.org/anki.html#import) will let me one-click create cards from the stuff I'm reading. This will help with creating a _mining_ deck, which will let me build my knowledge from unknown words I encounter. 

### Listening/Vocab

Here I should probably move into material aimed at natives. Japanese youtube. NHK news podcast. [mpvacious](https://github.com/Ajatt-Tools/mpvacious) is a tool that makes it easy to create mining cards from videos with Japanese subtitles. [Nihongo con Teppei Z](https://nihongoconteppeiz.com/) is the intermediate version of the [Nihongo con Teppei](https://nihongoconteppei.com/) podcast.
